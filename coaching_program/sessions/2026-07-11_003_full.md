**Why `🫡` is actually right:**
The whole method rests on the GM being frictionless and undramatic. A single
glyph reply embodies that:
- **Non-escalating.** Anything wordier ("Great job! Day 6, keep it up!")
  reintroduces exactly what Layer 3 exists to kill — the countdown, the audit,
  the sense of being graded. `🫡` acknowledges without evaluating.
- **Symmetric.** They send one token; you send one token. *Received, we're in
  this, carry on* — without making the exchange bigger than the ritual.
- **Sustainable for the coach.** Whatever scales for one client has to scale for
  fifty. A reply you'd resent typing on day 200 is the wrong reply.

**Where it's fragile — three gaps:**
1. "Reply to every GM" + a fixed emoji risks becoming a bot. If they ever write
   "GM — rough night, still here," an identical `🫡` reads as not-listening. The
   value of `🫡` is that it's the *default*, not the *only* thing you can send.
2. The plan says *what* to reply, never *why* — so a future coach can't tell when
   to deviate. `🫡` is a rule without a principle behind it. That's the actual
   defect.
3. It presumes the client reads `🫡` as "salute / respect / I see you." Emoji
   semantics aren't universal.

Recommendation: keep `🫡` as the standing default, but promote it from a bare
instruction to a stated principle — "acknowledge, never evaluate; deviate only
to match a client who added real content, never to praise or score."
